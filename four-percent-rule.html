The 4% Rule, Explained

The 4% rule is the most-cited guideline in retirement planning. Here's what it actually says, where it came from, when it holds up, and a quick calculator to apply it to your own numbers.

What the rule says

In your first year of retirement, withdraw 4% of your portfolio. Every year after, withdraw the same dollar amount adjusted for inflation — regardless of what the market does. Based on historical U.S. market data, a diversified portfolio following this plan has survived at least 30 years in every historical period tested, including retirements that started right before major crashes.

Where it comes from

The rule originates from financial planner William Bengen's 1994 research, later reinforced by the "Trinity Study" from three Trinity University professors. Both worked backward through decades of market history asking: what's the highest starting withdrawal rate that never exhausted a portfolio over a 30-year retirement? The answer landed around 4% for portfolios of roughly half stocks, half bonds.

Flipping it around: the 25× rule

The 4% rule is more often used in reverse, to set a savings target. If you can safely withdraw 4% a year, then you need 25 times your desired annual portfolio income saved. Want $40,000 a year from your investments? Target $1,000,000. That's exactly what our retirement number calculator computes, including adjustments for Social Security or pension income.

Limitations worth knowing

It's a guideline built on U.S. historical data, not a law of nature. It assumed a 30-year retirement (early retirees may want a lower rate — see the Coast FIRE calculator crowd's preference for 3.25%–3.5%), it ignores taxes and investment fees, and it assumes you'd never adjust spending in a bad market, which real retirees almost always do. Some researchers argue that flexibility makes rates above 4% workable; others argue lower future returns justify less. Test your own plan's endurance with the drawdown calculator.
